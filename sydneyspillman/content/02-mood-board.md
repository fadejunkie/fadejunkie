## Photography Direction

### Primary Style: Lifestyle Editorial
Warm, natural-light photography that puts people in real places. Not staged showroom shots — real life happening in real neighborhoods.

**Key characteristics:**
- Golden hour and soft daylight. No flash. No studio lighting.
- Families on porches, kids on bikes, morning coffee on a patio. Life happening.
- Interiors shot with lived-in warmth — natural textures, open windows, light falling across a kitchen counter.
- Sydney herself: candid, confident, approachable. Laughing with clients. Walking a neighborhood. Pointing at something off-camera. Never the stiff headshot-on-white-background.

**Photography references:**
- Kinfolk magazine — warm editorial lifestyle, desaturated slightly, human-centered
- Joanna Gaines / Magnolia editorial — warm interiors, real materials, inviting without being aspirational-cold
- Emily Henderson studio — bright, clean, accessible homes that feel like someone actually lives there

### Secondary Style: Architectural Context
Clean exterior shots of homes and neighborhoods — but always with context. A house on its street, not isolated on white. The oak tree out front. The neighborhood park across the road. The sidewalk.

**Purpose:** Ground the brand in real places. Sydney sells neighborhoods, not houses. The photography should reflect that.

### What to Avoid
- Drone shots of generic subdivisions (every agent uses these)
- Stock photography of any kind — especially the "couple receiving keys" cliche
- Dark, moody interior photography (signals luxury / not approachable)
- Over-saturated HDR real estate photography (looks cheap, feels transactional)

## Color Direction

### Foundation: White + Blue
The palette established in discovery — white base with blue accents — is the starting point. Here's how it expands.

### Primary Blue Range

| Role | Value | Usage |
|------|-------|-------|
| Primary blue | `#2563eb` | CTAs, links, key UI elements, logo accent |
| Light blue | `#3b82f6` | Hover states, secondary buttons, highlights |
| Deep navy | `#1e3a5f` | Footer, dense text blocks, contrast anchoring |

### Warm Neutrals (Expansion)

| Role | Value | Usage |
|------|-------|-------|
| Warm white | `#fafaf8` | Page backgrounds — not stark white, slightly warm |
| Light cream | `#f5f3ef` | Card backgrounds, section dividers, subtle depth |
| Warm gray | `#e8e5e0` | Borders, divider lines, inactive states |
| Mid gray | `#9c9890` | Secondary text, captions, metadata |
| Charcoal | `#2d2a26` | Primary text — warm black, not pure `#000000` |

**Why warm neutrals matter:** Pure white + blue reads clinical. A warm white base (`#fafaf8` instead of `#ffffff`) and warm grays (`#e8e5e0` instead of `#e5e7eb`) keep the approachable, lived-in feeling that matches Sydney's personality. The blue pops harder against warm backgrounds than cool ones.

### Accent Possibilities

| Accent | Value | Usage |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Soft gold | `#c9a96e` | Testimonial highlights, premium touches, subtle warmth |
| Sage | `#7c9082` | Success states, nature/community content markers |

**Guidance:** Accents should be used sparingly — a testimonial border, an icon fill, a small badge. The palette is blue + warm neutrals first. Accents add warmth without pulling focus.

## Typography Direction

### Display: Playfair Display (Serif)

Playfair Display carries the editorial authority. It says "real estate is serious" without saying "corporate."

| Context | Weight | Size Guidance | Example |
|---------|--------|---------------|---------|
| Hero headlines | 700 (Bold) | 48–64px desktop, 32–40px mobile | "Where you live matters." |
| Section headlines | 600 (SemiBold) | 32–40px desktop, 24–28px mobile | "Your neighborhood guide" |
| Card titles | 600 (SemiBold) | 20–24px | Listing titles, testimonial names |
| Pull quotes | 400 (Regular) Italic | 24–32px | Testimonial text, editorial callouts |

**Key rules:**
- Playfair at regular (400) italic for testimonial/editorial feels elegant and personal.
- Playfair never goes below 18px — it's a display face, not a reading face.
- Headline casing: sentence case, always. Never ALL CAPS (reads aggressive). Never Title Case On Every Word (reads template).

### Body + UI: Inter (Sans-Serif)

Inter handles everything that isn't a headline. It's invisible in the best way — clean, readable, professional without personality (that's the serif's job).

| Context | Weight | Size | Example |
|---------|--------|------|---------|
| Body text | 400 (Regular) | 16–18px | Paragraph copy, descriptions |
| Strong body | 500 (Medium) | 16–18px | Key phrases within body text |
| Labels/meta | 500 (Medium) | 12–14px | Form labels, card metadata, dates |
| Buttons/CTAs | 600 (SemiBold) | 14–16px | "Schedule a call", "View listing" |
| Eyebrows | 500 (Medium), uppercase, tracked | 11–12px | Section labels above headlines |

**Key rules:**
- Body text never goes below 16px on desktop — readability is non-negotiable.
- Button text at SemiBold 600, not Bold 700 — confident without shouting.
- Eyebrow labels: uppercase + wide letter-spacing (`0.08–0.12em`) creates the "editorial magazine section label" feel without needing a third typeface.

### Typographic Rhythm

The pairing creates a clear hierarchy:

```
EYEBROW — Inter 500 uppercase tracked
Headline — Playfair Display 700
Body text — Inter 400 at comfortable reading size
CTA — Inter 600 in a button
```

This rhythm should be consistent across every page, every section. The visitor should never wonder what's a headline vs. what's body text.

## Texture + Material References

### Overall Feel: Warm Paper
The brand should feel like high-quality print — a lifestyle magazine, not a digital-first SaaS product. Subtle paper textures, not glass and metal.

### Texture Direction

| Texture | Application | Guidance |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Linen / light paper grain** | Background overlays on hero sections, testimonial blocks | Very subtle — 2–4% opacity. The eye should feel warmth without seeing a pattern. |
| **Soft shadow / elevation** | Cards, photos, testimonial blocks | Cards float slightly — `0 2px 8px rgba(0,0,0,0.06)`. Not flat, not dramatic. |
| **Line dividers** | Section breaks | Thin (1px), warm gray (`#e8e5e0`). Clean separation. |
| **Rounded corners** | Cards, buttons, images | Generous but not bubbly — 12–16px radius. Friendly, not juvenile. |

### Material References
- Thick cream stationery — the kind you'd use for a handwritten thank-you note
- Light oak wood surfaces — warm, natural, grounding
- Linen fabric — soft texture, neutral warmth
- Natural stone (limestone, travertine) — San Antonio-native material, subtle prestige

### What to Avoid
- Glass morphism / frosted glass UI effects (too tech-forward)
- Dark mode as default (the brand is light and warm)
- Heavy gradients or neon accent colors (not in the brand DNA)
- Excessive border radius (40px+ feels app-like, not editorial)

---

## Mood Board Summary

**The brand should feel like:** Opening a beautifully designed lifestyle magazine about San Antonio neighborhoods — warm light on every page, real people in real places, clean type that lets the photography breathe, and a blue accent that says "trust me" without saying "corporate."

**The visitor should think:** "This feels different from every other agent's site. She actually cares about this."
