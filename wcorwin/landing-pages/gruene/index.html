New Braunfels' historic gem on the Guadalupe River

There's a stretch of the Guadalupe River where the water runs clear over limestone, the live oaks are older than anyone who lives beneath them, and the dance hall on the corner has been playing music since 1878. That's Gruene.

Officially part of New Braunfels, Gruene (pronounced "green") is listed on the National Register of Historic Places and home to Gruene Hall, the oldest continuously operating dance hall in Texas. But it's also a residential community with real roots, and it draws homebuyers who want a neighborhood that wasn't assembled in a planning office.

The story of Gruene

Gruene was founded in the 1870s by German immigrant families who farmed cotton along the Guadalupe River. The community thrived until the boll weevil and the Great Depression nearly erased it. By the 1970s, many of the original buildings were slated for demolition, until a group of preservationists stepped in to save them.

Today, the Gruene Historic District retains its original character: the dance hall, the general store, the old cotton gin — now the Gristmill River Restaurant — and streets that look much the way they did a century ago. That spirit carries through into the residential areas surrounding the district.

What it's like to live in Gruene

Gruene is a place where people wave from their porches. The residential areas surrounding the historic district range from established older homes on shaded lots to newer construction in communities like Cotton Crossing, Gruene Leaf, and Vineyard at Gruene.

The Guadalupe River runs along the western edge, and river access is one of the defining lifestyle features. Tubing, kayaking, and fishing are part of the daily rhythm in warmer months. Gruene Hall hosts live music year-round. The Gristmill River Restaurant is one of the best-known dining spots in the Hill Country.

Gruene is served by Comal ISD, with access to Gruene Elementary, Oak Creek Elementary, and New Braunfels High School. Daily conveniences are a 5 to 10 minute drive along Gruene Road or Hunter Road.

What to consider when buying near Gruene

  • Flood zone awareness

    The Guadalupe River floods. Properties near the river or in low-lying areas may fall within FEMA flood zones. Always verify flood zone status before making an offer. Flood insurance can add $1,500 to $3,000+ per year. The 2002 and 1998 flood events significantly impacted areas along the corridor.

  • Tourism traffic

    If you're buying within walking distance of the Historic District, expect increased traffic on weekends, holidays, and during major events — Gruene Music and Wine Fest, Thanksgiving weekend, etc. Most residents view this as a fair trade for the amenities. Experience a busy Saturday before committing to a specific street.

  • HOA vs. non-HOA

    Master-planned communities like Cotton Crossing and Vineyard at Gruene have HOAs with fees and architectural guidelines. Older areas generally do not. Short-term rental restrictions vary — check before buying if you plan to list on Airbnb.

  • Well and septic

    Some properties near Gruene are on city water and sewer. Others, particularly on larger lots, rely on well water and septic systems. Verify during due diligence — well inspection for flow rate and water quality, septic inspection for age and compliance.
